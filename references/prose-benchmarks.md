# Prose benchmarks

What this file is. A set of measurable properties of good plain prose, derived from five Paul Graham essays: Good Writing (2025), The Best Essay (2024), Do Things That Don't Scale (2013), Ramen Profitable (2009), How Y Combinator Started (2012). They were chosen because Graham both writes this way and, in Good Writing, explains the mechanics.

What this file is not. A voice to imitate. The named-author rule in SKILL.md stands: no profile may request "write like Paul Graham", and this file doesn't either. These are craft properties that show up in most good nonfiction prose; his essays just make them easy to observe and cite. Do not copy his mannerisms (the "Indeed," openers, the essay genre itself) into an email or a spec. Genre rules still govern.

Use these as a tuning fork for the skill's default voice, and as the reference when the final check asks "does this read human". When an author profile exists, the profile wins on any conflict.

## 1. The read-aloud test

Reread the finished text the way its reader will, and listen. Graham's claim in Good Writing: prose that sounds wrong usually has an idea wrong underneath, because rereading is where you catch both ("does anything catch?"). Operationally: any sentence you stumble on gets rewritten, and while rewriting, check whether the stumble was hiding a thinking error. It often is.

## 2. Sentence length must actually vary

Not "vary" as a vague aspiration. In his essays a 40-word sentence sits next to a 2-word one. From Do Things That Don't Scale, after a founder anecdote about sourcing screws: "Who knew?" Full sentence, full paragraph beat. The operational check: take any five consecutive sentences; if their lengths cluster within a narrow band, the rhythm is machine-regular. At least a few sentences per page should be under six words.

## 3. Slack sentences are allowed to exist

His essays contain sentences written at half pressure: "We'll see." "Yeah, that would be nice." "Or not, I don't know." These are not filler; they're the audible sound of a person thinking rather than a text being assembled. One or two per text. This is the direct antidote to clean slop, where every sentence is squeezed to maximum punch.

## 4. Uncertainty goes where the uncertainty is

He hedges constantly and precisely: "probably", "I think", "This could be just a taste of mine", "I don't know what ordinary typesetters do". The hedge marks a real epistemic state at the exact claim it applies to. Compare AI hedging, which is either absent (uniform confidence) or smeared over everything as insurance. The benchmark: confidence should be uneven across the text, because knowledge is.

## 5. Concreteness has a texture

Not just "use specifics" but a particular kind: physical, countable, slightly odd, remembered rather than invented. $3,000 a month. $100k plus $50k twice. Rolly bags at Tuesday dinners. Wet paint at the first California dinner. A 10% stake for Julian Weber. The oddness matters: a detail too smooth to be real ("the team gathered around a whiteboard") reads as generated. The epistemics section of SKILL.md still applies: you may only use details that exist.

## 6. One homely analogy per point, maximum

Crank engines before electric starters. Shaking a bin of objects. An elephant stepping into a rowboat. A fire kept contained to get hot. The pattern: one physical, domestic analogy that does real explanatory work, then back to the argument. Never two analogies for one idea, never abstract-on-abstract ("a paradigm functioning as a framework").

## 7. Questions do structural work

In The Best Essay, the essay literally advances by question and response, and sections open with questions ("Once you've got a question, then what?"). Questions are load-bearing: each one gets answered or explicitly deferred. Decorative rhetorical questions that the next sentence answers with a thud ("So what does this mean? It means...") are the AI version; cut those.

## 8. Self-correction can stay on the page

"Or rather we had the idea, but we didn't realize its significance." He revises himself mid-paragraph and leaves the seam visible. A model's instinct is to resolve everything before showing it. Occasionally showing the correction is both honest and human. Occasionally: once per text, where a real correction happened.

## 9. Endings without ceremony

The Best Essay ends on an open question. How Y Combinator Started ends on wet paint. Ramen Profitable ends on a caveat about consulting. None of them end on a recap, a lesson, or an aphorism. The last sentence should be the last thing you had to say, not a bow on the package.

## 10. Coin terms, then define them flat

"Ramen profitable means a startup makes just enough to pay the founders' living expenses." When a term is needed, it gets one plain definitional sentence, immediately, with edges ("It does not, for example, imply..."). No "refers to", no italicized mystique.

## 11. Cut without mercy, keep the cut visible if useful

He cut a 17-paragraph subtree from The Best Essay and told the reader so. The operational rule for the skill is already in the volume modes: in free-edit, deleting is the default treatment for weak material, not rewording. This benchmark adds: do not keep a passage because it cost effort, yours or the user's.

## Quick benchmark check

After the standard final check, ask:

1. Read five consecutive sentences anywhere. Do the lengths vary for real?
2. Is there at least one sentence written at half pressure?
3. Is confidence uneven, with hedges sitting on the actual soft spots?
4. Do the details have texture (countable, physical, slightly odd), and do they all exist?
5. Does anything end with a bow? Remove the bow.
