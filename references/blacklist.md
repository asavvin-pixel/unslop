# Stop lists: vocabulary and rhetoric of English AI text

Read in full before working on a text. This list does not declare words banned forever: a single occurrence can be fine. The problem starts where markers arrive in clusters or carry nothing. When rewriting, test every hit below with one question: "what is actually being said here?" If there is no answer, the phrase gets deleted, not paraphrased.

Shelf life warning. The lexical layer decays: "delve" peaked in 2023–2024 and collapsed in 2025; GPT-5.1 suppresses em dashes; each model generation has its own idiolect. Word lists below are dated where possible. The habits behind them (inflation, hedging-by-template, copula avoidance) are stable; target the habit, not just the word.

Sources: Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" essay, the UMD / Google DeepMind study (2026), editing practice.

## 1. Inflated significance and legacy

Markers:
- "stands/serves as a testament/reminder"
- "plays a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role"
- "underscores/highlights its importance/significance"
- "reflects broader (trends/debates/shifts)"
- "a key turning point", "a pivotal moment"
- "left an indelible mark", "enduring/lasting legacy"
- "deeply rooted", "focal point"
- "setting the stage for", "marking/shaping the"
- "has generated debate about", "has prompted broader reflection on"
- "cannot be overstated"

Fix: a fact from which the significance is visible on its own. Numbers, dates, consequences. No fact, no significance claim.

## 2. Promotional tone

Markers:
- "boasts a", "features a" (meaning "has")
- "rich (heritage/history/tapestry)", "vibrant", "renowned"
- "nestled in", "in the heart of", "breathtaking", "stunning natural beauty"
- "groundbreaking", "cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art", "innovative"
- "diverse array of", "wide range of"
- "commitment to excellence", "dedication to craftsmanship"
- "continues to captivate/inspire"
- "seamlessly", "effortlessly"

Fix: neutral description with specifics. "Operating since 2011", "12 offices", "main product is X".

## 3. Negative parallelisms

The whole family:
- "not just X, but Y" / "not only X, but also Y"
- "It's not X, it's Y" / "This isn't X; it's Y"
- "no X, no Y, just Z"
- "less about X than about Y"
- "X doesn't just A; it B"
- reversed, Grok-flavored: "prioritizing X rather than Y"
- multi-sentence: "One might think X. Instead, Y."

Fix: say what changed, why it matters, what data shows it. If Y really beats X, show Y and never mention X.

## 4. Rule of three

Forms:
- three adjectives: "fast, simple, and reliable"
- three parallel phrases: "plan, track, and analyze"
- three consecutive sentences with the same skeleton
- triads inside inflated-significance sentences ("ownership, consent, and dignity")

Fix: pick the one precise item and expand it, or write an honest list of however many items actually exist.

## 5. Superficial -ing analysis

Sign: a present-participle clause bolted to a fact, assigning it meaning the fact cannot carry. Facts don't "highlight" or "ensure"; people do.

Markers:
- "..., highlighting/underscoring/emphasizing ..."
- "..., reflecting/symbolizing ..."
- "..., ensuring ..."
- "..., showcasing/demonstrating ..."
- "..., contributing to ...", "..., fostering ..."
- "..., cementing its status as ..."
- "..., solidifying its role as ..."

Fix: split into two sentences. First: the fact. Second: what follows from it and per whose judgment, or nothing.

## 6. Vague attributions

Markers:
- "experts argue/believe/note"
- "observers have cited", "analysts suggest"
- "some critics argue"
- "industry reports show"
- "widely regarded/recognized as"
- "is described as" (by whom?)
- "several publications" (when one is cited)

Fix: name the source (outlet, report, person, date) or drop the claim. One source's view stays one source's view.

## 7. Didactic disclaimers

Markers:
- "it's important/crucial/critical to note/remember/consider"
- "worth noting/mentioning"
- "it should be noted that"
- "keep in mind that"
- "one must remember"
- "results may vary"

Fix: delete the disclaimer, keep the content. "It's important to note the timeline may slip" becomes "The timeline may slip."

## 8. AI vocabulary (era-dependent)

High-frequency LLM words. One or two are coincidence; a cluster is one of the strongest tells. Roughly by era:

2023 to mid-2024 (GPT-4 era):
- delve, tapestry (abstract), testament, intricate/intricacies, meticulous(ly), pivotal, crucial, garner, bolster, interplay, vibrant, boasts, landscape (abstract), enduring, "Additionally," (sentence-initial), key (adjective)

Mid-2024 to mid-2025 (GPT-4o era):
- align with, enhance, fostering, highlighting, showcasing, underscore, emphasizing, robust, vibrant, pivotal

Mid-2025 onward (GPT-5 era):
- emphasizing, enhance, highlighting, showcasing; heavier reliance on sourcing-emphasis phrasing ("independent coverage", "profiled in")

Grok-specific: causal, empirical, correlate, underscore (still, as of 2026).

Fix: the plain word. Explore, mix, proof, detailed, careful, important, get, support, and, has. Note: a synonym of an AI word is not itself suspect; this list is literal.

## 9. Copula avoidance

Sign: the model dodges plain "is/are/has" and definitional simplicity.

Markers:
- "serves as", "stands as", "acts as", "functions as"
- "marks", "represents" (meaning "is")
- "features", "offers", "boasts", "maintains" (meaning "has")
- "refers to" in a lead definition ("X refers to the practice of...")
- "holds the distinction of being"
- "ventured into politics as a candidate" (was a candidate), "began his career as" (was)

Fix: is, are, has. "The gallery is the exhibition space. It has four rooms." In definitions: "X is...", not "X refers to...".

## 10. Synonym cycling

Sign: the text refuses to repeat a name or term and rotates substitutes: "the protagonist", "the key player", "the central figure", "the celebrated author". Cause: repetition penalty. Humans repeat words without embarrassment.

Fix: repeat the name or term. A pronoun if the referent is unambiguous. (Caveat: some human writers, and some school traditions, also drill elegant variation; treat a light touch of it as human, a mechanical rotation as AI.)

## 11. Mechanical connectives

Markers:
- "Additionally," / "Moreover," / "Furthermore," (especially sentence-initial, in rotation)
- "Consequently," "Thus," "Hence," in every other paragraph
- "In today's fast-paced/digital/ever-evolving world"
- "In the realm/landscape of"
- "When it comes to"
- "At its core"
- "It is worth mentioning that"

Fix: "and", "but", "so", "which is why", or nothing. Often the best transition is the next thought with no connective at all.

## 12. Dramatization

Markers:
- "game-changer", "changes the game"
- "revolutionize", "radically transform"
- "take X to the next level"
- "unlock the full potential"
- "a paradigm shift"
- "poised to reshape"
- "the future of X is here"

Fix: "may help", "reduces the dependency on", "adds another channel", "makes X cheaper to test".

## 13. Fake casualness

Markers (inserted folksiness simulating a voice):
- "Honestly," / "Let's be honest" / "Let's be real"
- "Here's the thing:"
- "Spoiler:", "Spoiler alert:"
- "the secret sauce"
- "Plot twist:"
- "And here's the kicker:"
- "In plain English:" (when nothing was complicated)

Fix: none. Voice comes from a precise thought and a concrete detail.

## 14. Self-summary

Forms:
- a "Conclusion" / "In summary" / "Overall" section restating the piece
- a paragraph whose last sentence explains what the paragraph meant
- "In summary,", "To sum up,", "All in all,"
- "Ultimately, X is more than just Y" (combines with negative parallelism)

Fix: delete. Keep an ending only if it adds something new: a decision, a next step, a consequence.

## 15. Challenges-and-prospects template

Sign: the rigid formula, not any mention of difficulty.

Markers:
- "Despite its X, [subject] faces several challenges..."
- "Despite these challenges, [subject] continues to..."
- "Challenges and Future Outlook/Prospects/Directions" sections
- a vaguely positive final assessment after a challenges list
- speculation about "potential future developments" with no source

Fix: end where the substance ends. If real risks exist, name them concretely, with owners and magnitudes, and stop.

## 16. Empty merisms

Sign: "from X to Y" where no real scale runs between the poles.

Markers:
- "from startups to enterprises"
- "from concept to launch"
- "from beginners to professionals"
- "from landing pages to high-load systems"

Fix: list what you actually mean, or name the one thing that matters.

## 17. Overgeneralized sourcing

Markers:
- one review rendered as "reviewers note"
- two articles rendered as "widespread/extensive coverage"
- "such as" implying a longer list the sources never gave
- "multiple outlets", "several scholars" with one citation
- inflating trivial mentions into "has been featured in"

Fix: keep the count honest. "One reviewer", "two trade articles", the actual names.

## 18. Chatbot artifacts

Sign: service phrases from the chat session leaked into the text. Delete silently when rewriting.

Markers:
- "Certainly!", "Of course!", "I hope this helps", "Let me know if..."
- "You're absolutely right"
- "As of my last training update", "as of my knowledge cutoff"
- "in the provided search results", "based on available information"
- "While specific details are limited/scarce..."
- "Would you like me to..."
- "In this article, we will explore..."
- placeholders: "[Your Name]", "[Describe the specific section...]", "2025-XX-XX"
- markup debris: `utm_source=chatgpt.com`, `utm_source=openai`, `referrer=grok.com`, `turn0search0`, `oaicite`, `oai_citation`, `[cite: 3]`, `attributableIndex`, stray ``` fences, `---` breaks before headings
